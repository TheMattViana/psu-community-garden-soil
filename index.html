What 13 soil samples revealed

Your garden is richer than it needs to be.

We sent samples from 13 plots to Penn State's Agricultural Analytical Lab. The results came back clear and consistent. Phosphorus is at four times the recommended level. Calcium and magnesium are at twice. Organic matter is at twice. The most useful thing you can do for your soil this season is leave it alone.

Here's what the numbers say — and what they mean for what you plant.

Why this is happening

Soil is a pantry, not a refrigerator.

A refrigerator empties every week — milk, lettuce, leftovers gone. A pantry, full of dry goods, doesn't. Compost and fertilizer behave like the pantry: they don't get used up the same season they're added. Year after year of well-meaning amendments build up.

By the time the soil is saying "I'm overflowing," the buildup is the work of many gardeners over many years. None of it is anyone's fault — it's how community gardens almost always end up.

Adding more compost now is like piling flour into a pantry that already won't close.

Your plan this season

Five things, in order.

If you only do these, the garden will be healthier next year than it was this year.

  1. Skip the compost and fertilizer

    Your plot already has more nutrients than crops can use. Adding more locks up iron and zinc, and runoff pollutes nearby waterways.

  2. Plant heavy feeders

    Brassicas (broccoli, kale, cabbage), leafy greens, tomatoes, asparagus. They thrive in rich soil and pull excess nutrients out.

  3. Pull plants at season end — don't chop and drop

    Remove plant material from your plot and add it to the central compost pile. Don't recycle nutrients straight back into already-saturated soil.

  4. Optional: a small copper sulfate amendment

    Copper is the one nutrient slightly low relative to zinc. A modest amendment to ~4–5 ppm is the only fertilizer this garden needs. Talk to the coordinator first.

  5. Plant a cereal cover crop after harvest

    Winter rye, oats, or wheat sown in fall scavenge nutrients, prevent erosion, and add structure (not more nutrients) when terminated next spring.

What to remember
  • Skip the compost and fertilizer this year — there's already plenty in the soil.
  • Plant heavy feeders. Avoid root crops and acid-loving plants.
  • Cover crop with cereal grains in fall to keep nutrients on-site over winter.
